Chapter 2: Exercises

Exercise 1

Suppose $f$ is a $2 \pi $-periodic and integrable function on any finite interval. Prove that if $ a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, then \[ \int_a^b f(x) = \int_{a + 2\pi}^{b + 2 \pi} f(x) = \int_{a - 2 \pi}^{b - 2 \pi} f(x). \] Also prove that \[ \int_{- \pi}^\pi f(x + a) dx = \int_{- \pi}^\pi f(x) dx = \int_{-\pi + a}^{\pi + a} f(x) dx. \]

Answer

Graphically, the first claim is obvious. Since the function itself is invariant under shifts of $2 \pi$, then the area under the curve must also be invariant under shifts of $2 \pi$. Consider the special case $f(x) = \sin(x)$ shown in the interactive diagram below. Drag $a$ and $b$ and observe how the area of the region under the curve stays constant. We justify the first claim with two changes of variables: $x = x + 2 \pi$ and $x = x - 2 \pi$. The first change gives \[ \int_a^b f(x) dx = \int_{a + 2\pi}^{b + 2\pi} f(x - 2\pi) dx. \] The periodicity of $f$ gives $f(x - 2\pi) = f(x)$ so \[ \int_a^b f(x) dx = \int_{a + 2 \pi}^{b + 2\pi} f(x) dx \] as desired. Similarly, we can deduce that \[ \int_a^b f(x) dx = \int_{a - 2 \pi}^{b - 2\pi} f(x). \] The second statement is not quite so clear. The following graphic shows that integrating from $- \pi + a$ to $\pi + a$ is the same as integrating from $-\pi$ to $\pi$ by periodicity. The shaded parts show the missing area when integrating over the two different intervals, but note that they are the same. Symbolically, we can rewrite the integral as \[ \int_{-\pi + a}^{\pi + a} f(x) dx = \int_{-\pi + a}^\pi f(x)dx + \int_\pi^{\pi + a} f(x) dx. \] It then remains to show that \[ \int_\pi^{\pi + a} f(x) dx = \int_{-\pi}^{-\pi + a} f(x) dx. \] Using the first statement of the question with $a = -\pi$ and $b = -\pi + a$, we have as desired \[ \int_{-\pi}^{-\pi + a} f(x) dx = \int_\pi^{\pi + a} f(x) dx. \]
