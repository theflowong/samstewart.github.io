Part A

    • Define the fundamental group $\pi_1(X, x)$ of a space with basepoint

      See what is the fundamental group?.

      Let $(X, x)$ be a topological space with a basepoint, and $f, g$ be two loops at that basepoint. Consider the equivalence relation $\sim$ where $f \sim g$ if and only if there is a homotopy between $f$ and $g$. Let $G$ be the group of loops at $x$ with the operation of concatenation. Then we define \[ \pi_1(X, x) = G / \sim \] This object is a group.

    • Show that there is an isomorphism \[ \pi_1(X \times Y, (x, y)) \to \pi_1(X, x) \times \pi_1(Y, y). \]

      A loop in the product space is also a loop in each coordinate.
      We claim the following is an isomorphism $\Phi : \pi_1(X \times Y, (x, y)) \to \pi_1(X, x) \times \pi_1(Y, y)$ \[ \Phi([\gamma]) = ([P_X(\gamma)], \, [P_Y(\gamma)]) \] with $P_X, P_Y$ projections onto $X$ and $Y$. We sketch the justification

      • Well-defined

        The projections are continuous, so composing them with homotopies produces another homotopy.

      • Homomorphism

        The projections are homomorphisms under concatenation.

        • Surjective

        Any path in $X$ or $Y$ is a path in $X \times Y$ with the do-nothing path in the opposite coordinate.

      • Injective

        If a path in $X \times Y$ is homotopic to zero in $X$ and $Y$ individually, then we can combine the two homotopies into a new homotopy for the whole path to zero by homotopying the first coordinate to zero, and then the second.

    • If $a,b, p$ are distinct elements in $\R^2$, describe the fundamental group $\pi_1(\R^2 \backslash \{ a, b \})$ in terms of generators and relations.

      The doubly punctured plane deformation retracts to $S^1 \vee S^1$ and thus has fundamental group \[ \Z * \Z = < a, b >. \]
      We can construct a special map called a deformation retract between $\R^2 \backslash \{ a, b \}$ and $S^1 \vee S^1$.
      Let $A$ be a subset of $X$. We call $A$ a deformation retract of $X$ if there is a homotopy between a retract of $X$ into $A$ and the identity map. More precisely, $A$ is a deformation retract of $X$ if there exists a homotopy $H : X \times [0, 1] \to X$ such that
      • $H(x,0) = x$
      • $H(a, t) = a$ for $a \in A$
      • $H(x, 1) \in A$ for all $x \in X$
      The earlier interactive graphic describes this map. We then apply the following theorem from Munkres (58.3, p 361)
      Let $A$ be a deformation retract of $X$; let $x_0 \in A$. Then the inclusion map \[ j :(A, x_0) \to (X, x_0) \] induces an isomorphism of fundamental groups.
      One can prove with Van Kampen Theorem that $\pi_1(S^1 \vee S^1) = < a, b >$. With the above theorem, this implies that $\pi_1(\R^2 \backslash \{ a, b \}) = < a, b >$.

    • Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are spaces (not necessarily connected). Define what it means for a map $p : Y \to X$ to be a covering map.

      See what are covering spaces?.
      Let $p : Y \to X$ be a continuous surjective map. Then we say $Y$ is a covering space of $X$ if for any $x_0 \in X$ there exists a neighborhood $U$ such that \[ p^{-1}(U) = \bigcup_\alpha V_\alpha \] where the $V_\alpha$ are pairwise disjoint and open in $Y$. If this condition holds, we call $p$ a covering map and say that $x_0$ is evenly covered.

    • Define the degree of a continuous self-map $f : S^n \to S^n$.

      Intuitively, the degree of a map is an integer that counts the number of times it winds one loop around the circle. Thus, the degree of $z \to z^n$ is $n$, the degree of the identity is $1$, and the degree of a reflection is $-1$, the degree of the antipodal map is $1$ (it is the composition of two reflections). See computing the fundamental group of the circle.
      Let $f : S^n \to S^n$ be a continuous self map of the circle. Then we have an induced automorphism $f^*$ on $H^n(S^n) = \Z$. Since $\Z$ is cyclic, the map $f^*$ is uniquely determined by $f^*(1) = n$. We call $n$ the degree of $f$.

    • If $f$ and $g$ are continuous self-maps of $S^n$, determine the degree of $g \circ f$.

      Composition simply speeds up the trajectory around the circle. For example, if $f(z) = z^2$ with degree two and $g(z) = z^3$ with degree three then $g \circ f = z^6$ with degree $6 = 3 \circ 2$. The degree is clearly not additive since $f = g = z$ gives $f \circ g = z$ with degree $1 = 1 \cdot 1 \neq 1 + 1 = 2$.
      By the properties of the functor between topological spaces and groups, we know that $g \circ f$ induces a map $(g \circ f)^* = g^* \circ f^*$. Then by definition the degree of this map is \[ \begin{aligned} (g^* \circ f^*)(1) &= g^*(f^*(1)) \cr &= g^*(\textrm{deg}(f)) \cr &= \textrm{deg}(f) g^*(1) \cr &= \textrm{deg}(f) \textrm{deg}(g) \end{aligned} \]

Part B

    • Give the definition of a smooth coordinate atlas on a manifold $M$.

      A smooth atlas is nothing but a collection of maps from open sets of the manifold $M$ to open sets of $\R^n$. A manifold is simply a collection of copies of $\R^n$ stitched together. As an example, consider $S^1$ divided into three sets with the projection maps on each set.
      Let $U_\alpha$ be a collection of open sets that cover $M$. We call $\phi_\alpha : U_\alpha \to \R^n$ a smooth atlas if
      • $\phi_\alpha$ is a homeomorphism between $U_\alpha$ and $\phi_\alpha(U_\alpha)$
      • If $U_\alpha \cap U_\beta \neq \emptyset$, then $\phi_\alpha \circ \phi_\beta^{-1} : \phi_beta(U_\alpha \cap U_\beta) \to \phi_\alpha(U_\alpha \cap U_\beta)$ is a diffeomorphism (compatability condition)

    • Give a (coordinate-free) definition of a smooth vector field on $M$ (assuming the definition of a smooth real-valued function is given).

      A smooth vector field is simply a vector field where each coordinate is locally smooth. For example, the vector field $(y, -x)$ smoothly swirls around the origin in the manifold $\R^2$
      The definition of a smooth vector field depends on two dependent ideas.
      For manifolds $M$ and $N$, we call a map $F : M \to N$ smooth if for every set of smooth charts $(\phi, U), (\xi, V)$ where $U \subset M, V \subset N$ we have that $\xi \circ F \circ \phi^{-1} : \phi(U \cap F^{-1}(V) \to \phi(V)$ is a smooth map.
      The tangent bundle $TM$ of a manifold $M$ is defined by \[ TM = \coprod_p T_p M. \]
      A smooth vector field on a manifold $M$ is a smooth map $F : M \to TM$ such that $F(p) = X_p \in T_p M$.
