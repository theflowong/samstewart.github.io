Why do we need covering spaces?

What is a covering space?

There is a simple model we will use to motivate the definition of a covering space. If we convert $\R$ into a helix centered at $0$, then the vertical projection of a point gives a point on $S^1$ embedded in the plane.

Intuitively, the larger object $\R$ lies above $S^1$ and collapses nicely to the circle. The definition of a covering space requires two different spaces: the covering space $E$, the base space $B$, and a map $p : E \to B$ relating the two spaces. The constraints shown in the above figure are thus really constraints on $p$ (see relative definitions in mathematics).

From our example, its clear that $E$ should completely cover $B$ and should locally preserve the topological structure of $B$ (no tearing). In other words, $p$ should be surjective and continuous. But a simple projection satisfies this definition; we want another constraint on the structure of the covering space $E$: we want $E$ to look like copies of $E$ glued nicely together. How do we capture this idea formally? We demand that the topology of $E$ is a set of copies of the topology of $B$.

More precisely, for $p \in B$, there must exist a neighborhood $U$ of $p$ such that $p^{-1}(U)$ is equal to a union of disjoint sets $V_\alpha$ open in $E$. Each $V_\alpha$ is a (maybe deformed) copy of $U$ in $E$. But we don't want mild deformation, so we also require that each $V_\alpha$ is homeomorphic to $U$ under $p$.

In the following interactive diagram, you can adjust the endpoints of the open neighborhood of $(1, 0)$ and see the corresponding lifted $V_\alpha$'s in $E$.

Our precise definition is thus

Let $E$ and $B$ be topological spaces and $p : E \to B$ a continuous and surjective map between them. If for every $p \in B$ there is a neighborhood $U$ such that $p^{-1}(U)$ is the disjoint union of open sets $V_\alpha$ in $E$ and each $V_\alpha$ is homeomorphic to $E$ under $p$, then we say $p$ is a covering map and $E$ is a covering space for $B$.

Products of covering maps

We can build covering maps of product spaces by simply covering each component space. As an example, consider $T^2 = S^1 \times S^1$ with the covering space $\R^2$ and covering map \[ p(\theta, \phi) = (p_1(\theta), p_2(\phi)) \] where $p_1, p_2$ are the covering map of $S^1$ by $\R$ that we discussed in the last section. There are a few other useful examples. We can construct a trivial covering space for any topological space $X$ by simply stacking copies of $X$ above itself. Formally, the space $X \times \{ 1, \ldots, n \}$ is a covering space of $X$ if imbued with the covering map $p(x, i) = x$. Of course, with $n = 1$, this implies that $X$ covers itself. The second example is more complicated and is an application to complex analysis. In $\C$, the log function has a branch point at $z = 0$ where, if not properly restricted, the function is multivalued. However, if we change the domain to copies of $\C \ \{ \R^- \}$ glued together along $\R^-$ to form a helical cover of $\C$, then $\log(z)$ becomes analytic.

Are local homeomorphisms also covering maps?

We call a map $f : X \to Y$ a local homeomorphism if every point $p \in X$ has a neighborhood $U$ so that $f$ restricted to $U$ is a homeomorphism to $f(U)$. Every covering map $p : E \to B$ is a local homeomorphism. To see why, let $x \in E$. Then by definition of a covering space, the point $p(x)$ has a neighborhood $V$ in $B$ such that $p^{-1}(V)$ is a disjoint union of open $U_\alpha$ which are individually homeomorphic to $V$. One of the $U_\alpha$'s must contain $x$, so we are done.

On the other hand, not every local homeomorphism is a covering map. In some sense, a covering map is a collection of local homeomorphisms. As a counter example, consider again the covering map \[ p(\theta) = (\cos(2 \pi \theta), \sin (2 \pi \theta)) \] of $S^1$ by $\R$. What is the difference between a quotient and covering space?

The ideas are similar: both involve collapsing a larger object onto a smaller, and both ideas have similar definitions: both involve maps.
